\begin{abstract}
Physics-informed machine learning promises data-efficient, physically
consistent dynamics models, but it is often unclear \emph{when} a physics prior
pays off relative to a black-box baseline---or whether a mismatched prior can
hurt. We present a controlled, reproducible benchmark of seven learned dynamics
models---a multilayer perceptron (MLP), a polynomial system-identification
baseline, a Gaussian process (GP), a Lagrangian Neural Network (LNN), a naive
Hamiltonian Neural Network (HNN), a structured HNN with a learned mass matrix,
and a Lagrangian-informed GP (LGP)---on two underactuated systems, the Acrobot
and the Pendubot. We evaluate four axes: one-step accuracy, long-horizon rollout
stability (valid prediction time), energy drift, and downstream energy-based
swing-up control, across five seeds, three data budgets, and both clean and
sensor/process-noise conditions. We find a nuanced map rather than a single
winner. The LNN, whose learned positive-definite mass matrix matches these
systems, is the most accurate and energy-consistent and never diverges. The GP
is the most data-efficient and yields the most stable rollouts despite modest
one-step accuracy. The LGP blends the two, achieving LNN-level accuracy with
GP-level rollout stability and the lowest energy drift. The naive HNN, whose
identity-mass assumption is violated by configuration-dependent inertia, is
worse than the black-box MLP and diverges in $20$--$32\%$ of rollouts; a
controlled ablation shows that giving the Hamiltonian model a learned mass
matrix---which by Legendre duality recovers the LNN---fully removes this failure,
isolating the assumption rather than the formulation as the cause. Under noise
all models degrade gracefully, but the GP residual in the LGP overfits noise and
loses rollout stability. Only models with an energy function can drive
energy-based control. The result is a concrete map of when physics priors help:
scarce data, long horizons and energy-sensitive tasks, and a prior whose
structure matches the system.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Learning the dynamics of a mechanical system from data underpins model-based
control and reinforcement learning. Plain neural networks fit one-step
transitions accurately, yet rolled out over long horizons they drift and violate
invariants such as energy conservation. Physics-informed machine learning (PiML)
bakes structure---Lagrangian or Hamiltonian mechanics, positive-definite
inertia, energy conservation---into the model \cite{karniadakis2021,raissi2019},
promising better long-horizon behaviour and sample efficiency.

In practice it is rarely obvious \emph{when} a prior is worth its complexity, or
whether a prior whose assumptions are violated by the real system can hurt. Most
papers introduce one physics-informed model and show it beats a baseline on a
favourable task; what is missing is a controlled, like-for-like comparison across
priors, evaluation criteria, data budgets, and noise conditions. Underactuated
systems such as the Acrobot and Pendubot are an ideal stress test: they are
low-dimensional yet chaotic, have configuration-dependent inertia, and admit a
demanding downstream control task (swing-up) that depends on the learned model's
physical consistency, not just its one-step error.

We provide such a comparison and summarise it as a map of when physics priors
help, with four findings:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Matching structure matters most.} The LNN, which learns a
positive-definite, configuration-dependent mass matrix, is the most accurate and
energy-consistent model and never diverges.
\item \textbf{A mismatched prior is worse than none.} The naive HNN, assuming an
identity mass matrix, is worse than the black-box MLP and diverges in
$20$--$32\%$ of rollouts. A controlled ablation pinpoints the assumption: giving
the Hamiltonian model a learned mass matrix removes the failure.
\item \textbf{Uncertainty buys stability and data efficiency.} The GP is the most
data-efficient model and yields the most stable rollouts; combining it with
Lagrangian structure (the LGP) gives the best overall blend on clean data.
\item \textbf{Robustness has costs.} Under noise the GP is the most robust, while
the LGP's GP residual overfits noise and loses rollout stability.
\end{enumerate}
Our contribution is the controlled study and the resulting map, together with a
released, configuration-driven pipeline that regenerates every result.

\section{Related Work}
\textbf{Physics-informed neural dynamics.} Deep Lagrangian Networks (DeLaN)
\cite{lutter2019} learn the mass matrix and potential of a Lagrangian and recover
the equations of motion by automatic differentiation; Lagrangian Neural Networks
\cite{cranmer2020} generalise the idea to an arbitrary learned Lagrangian.
Hamiltonian Neural Networks \cite{greydanus2019} learn a Hamiltonian and
integrate the symplectic flow, conserving energy by construction but requiring
canonical coordinates; symplectic and variational-integrator variants extend them
to control and rigid bodies \cite{zhong2020,saemundsson2020}. Physics-informed
neural networks more broadly embed governing equations into the loss
\cite{raissi2019,karniadakis2021}.

\textbf{Gaussian-process and classical dynamics.} GPs provide calibrated
uncertainty and data efficiency for model learning \cite{rasmussen2006,nguyen2011}
and underpin sample-efficient control such as PILCO \cite{deisenroth2011}. Sparse
regression on a feature library (SINDy) is a classical system-identification
baseline \cite{brunton2016}. Hybrid schemes learn a data-driven residual on top of
a parametric or structured model; our Lagrangian-informed GP is an instance with a
learned-Lagrangian mean.

\textbf{Underactuated swing-up.} The Acrobot and Pendubot are standard benchmarks;
energy-shaping controllers swing them toward upright by regulating total energy,
typically with an LQR catch for final balancing \cite{spong1995,tedrake}. We use
swing-up as a downstream task that stresses the \emph{usefulness} of a learned
model, not only its predictive error.

Relative to this literature we hold data, integrator, training objective, and
evaluation fixed across seven models and report multiple axes, data budgets and
noise conditions, enabling a fair comparison rather than a single favourable
demonstration.

\section{Background and Problem Setup}
\subsection{Underactuated double pendulums}
We study two planar double pendulums with a single actuator. Their dynamics follow
the manipulator equation
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:manip}
M(q)\,\ddot q + C(q,\dot q)\,\dot q + g(q) = B\,u,
\end{equation}
with joint angles $q=(q_1,q_2)$, symmetric positive-definite mass matrix
$M(q)\succ 0$, Coriolis/centrifugal term $C(q,\dot q)$, gravity
$g(q)=\partial V/\partial q$ derived from a potential $V(q)$, and actuation matrix
$B\in\mathbb{R}^2$. The \textbf{Acrobot} is actuated only at the elbow
($B=[0,1]^\top$) and the \textbf{Pendubot} only at the shoulder ($B=[1,0]^\top$);
the remaining joint is passive, making both systems underactuated. The state is
$x=(q_1,q_2,\dot q_1,\dot q_2)$ and the control $u$ is a scalar torque. The total
mechanical energy is $E(x)=\tfrac12\dot q^\top M(q)\dot q + V(q)$, which is
conserved when $u=0$. We integrate the true dynamics with a fourth-order
Runge--Kutta (RK4) scheme at $\Delta t=10^{-2}\,$s.

\subsection{Lagrangian and Hamiltonian structure}
The same dynamics admit two structured parameterizations that the models below
exploit. The Lagrangian $\mathcal{L}=\tfrac12\dot q^\top M(q)\dot q - V(q)$ yields
the Euler--Lagrange equations, equivalent to \eqref{eq:manip} with
$C\dot q = \dot M\dot q - \tfrac12\nabla_q(\dot q^\top M\dot q)$. The Hamiltonian
$H(q,p)=\tfrac12 p^\top M(q)^{-1}p + V(q)$, with canonical momentum $p=M(q)\dot q$,
yields Hamilton's equations $\dot q=\partial H/\partial p$,
$\dot p=-\partial H/\partial q + Bu$. The two are related by the Legendre
transform, so a model that learns $M(q)$ and $V(q)$ produces the \emph{same}
equations of motion whether written in Lagrangian or Hamiltonian form---a fact we
use to construct a fair Hamiltonian baseline.

\subsection{Learning problem}
Given transitions $(x_t,u_t,x_{t+1})$ collected under random torques, the task is
to learn $\hat f$ predicting the state derivative (hence $x_{t+1}$ via the same
RK4 integrator). A single integrator and one-step objective are used for every
model, so behavioural differences are attributable to the prior, not the training
setup.

\section{The Model Zoo}
All models map $(x,u)$ to a state derivative and are rolled out with RK4. Neural
models use two hidden layers of $64$ units; Lagrangian/Hamiltonian models use
Softplus activations (twice differentiable, required for the autograd-derived
forces).

\textbf{Vanilla MLP (black box).} A $128$-$128$-$128$ network regresses joint
accelerations directly; no physical structure.

\textbf{Polynomial (system ID).} Closed-form ridge regression of accelerations on
a degree-2 polynomial expansion of the features
$\phi=[\sin q_1,\cos q_1,\sin q_2,\cos q_2,\dot q_1,\dot q_2,u]$; a cheap classical
baseline ($\ell_2$ penalty $10^{-4}$).

\textbf{Gaussian Process (GP).} An exact GP with an ARD-RBF kernel regresses each
acceleration component from $\phi$, fit by marginal likelihood; the $(\sin,\cos)$
encoding respects angle periodicity. We cap the training set at $1000$ points for
tractability of the $O(n^3)$ solve.

\textbf{Lagrangian Neural Network (LNN).} Two networks output a Cholesky factor of
$M(q)=L(q)L(q)^\top+\epsilon I\succ 0$ and a potential $V(q)$; the Coriolis and
gravity terms follow by automatic differentiation and the acceleration from
\eqref{eq:manip}. The known $B$ is supplied as a prior.

\textbf{Naive HNN.} A scalar Hamiltonian $H(q,p)$ with the identity-mass
convention $p:=\dot q$, integrated along the symplectic flow. It does \emph{not}
learn a configuration-dependent mass matrix.

\textbf{Structured HNN.} A Hamiltonian model that \emph{learns} $M(q)$ and $V(q)$
with canonical momentum $p=M(q)\dot q$. By the Legendre transform its equations of
motion coincide with the LNN's; we include it as a controlled ablation that
isolates the naive HNN's identity-mass assumption.

\textbf{Lagrangian-informed GP (LGP).} A hybrid: an LNN supplies a physics mean
and a GP models the residual acceleration, $\ddot q = \ddot q_{\mathrm{LNN}} +
\mathrm{GP}_{\mathrm{res}}$, keeping structure while letting the GP correct what
the structural model misses.

\subsection{Structured dynamics in detail}
The LNN and Structured HNN share the same learned ingredients. To guarantee a
physically valid, invertible inertia, the mass matrix is parameterized through a
Cholesky factor,
\begin{equation}
M(q) = L(q)L(q)^\top + \epsilon I,
\end{equation}
where $L(q)$ is lower-triangular with positive (Softplus) diagonal and
$\epsilon=10^{-2}$ floors the smallest eigenvalue for a well-conditioned solve.
Given $M(q)$ and $V(q)$, the gravity and Coriolis terms are obtained by automatic
differentiation,
\begin{equation}
g(q)=\nabla_q V,\qquad
[C(q,\dot q)\dot q]_i = (\dot M\dot q)_i - \tfrac12\,\dot q^\top
   \frac{\partial M}{\partial q_i}\dot q,
\end{equation}
with $\dot M = \sum_k (\partial M/\partial q_k)\,\dot q_k$, and the acceleration
solves $M\ddot q = Bu - C\dot q - g$. The Structured HNN evaluates the
algebraically equivalent Hamiltonian form
$\ddot q = M^{-1}\!\left(-\partial H/\partial q|_p + Bu - \dot M\dot q\right)$;
both conserve their own learned energy to $\sim\!10^{-7}$ over $200$ steps even
before training, confirming the correctness of the autograd equations of motion.
The naive HNN omits $M(q)$, setting $p:=\dot q$.

\section{Evaluation Protocol}
\textbf{Data.} Per system we generate $8000$ transitions from random short
rollouts under uniform random torques; a $20\%$ tail is held out for one-step
evaluation and the remaining pool is randomly subsampled to budgets of
$250,1000,4000$ to probe data efficiency. The \emph{noisy} condition adds process
noise---a Gaussian state kick after each step, modelling unmodelled forces---and
sensor noise added to the recorded states, both with standard deviation $0.02$.

\textbf{Training.} Each model minimises a one-step angle-aware MSE between the
RK4-integrated prediction and the recorded next state (GP-style models are instead
fit by marginal likelihood). The angle-aware loss wraps angular errors to
$[-\pi,\pi]$ so the $\pm\pi$ discontinuity does not corrupt the gradient. We use
$100$ epochs, Adam, gradient clipping, and \emph{five} seeds per configuration.

\textbf{Implementation.} The MLP has three $128$-unit ReLU layers; the structured
networks use two $64$-unit Softplus layers each for $M(q)$ and $V(q)$. We train
with Adam (learning rate $10^{-3}$, batch size $256$) and clip gradient norms to
$10$. The GP uses an ARD-RBF kernel with per-feature lengthscales and signal/noise
variances optimised by $25$ marginal-likelihood steps in double precision; the LGP
fits its residual GP on the same capped subsample. Process and sensor noise are
zero-mean Gaussian (std $0.02$) on the four state components, and a rollout is
declared diverged if any state exceeds $10^{2}$ in magnitude or becomes
non-finite. All experiments run on CPU.

\textbf{Metrics.} (1)~\emph{One-step MSE} on the held-out set.
(2)~\emph{Valid prediction time}: the number of steps an autoregressive free-swing
($u\!=\!0$) rollout tracks the truth before the per-step error first exceeds a
fixed threshold. On chaotic systems raw long-horizon MSE saturates (two nearby
trajectories decorrelate and the error reaches a ceiling), so it cannot
discriminate models; valid prediction time does. (3)~\emph{Energy drift}: the
range of the true energy $E(x)$ along the model's free rollout, which should be
zero; we also report the fraction of rollouts that diverge (state magnitude or
non-finite values exceeding a bound). (4)~\emph{Swing-up control}
(Section~\ref{sec:control}). All rollout evaluations use identical fixed initial
conditions across models for fairness.

The LNN is the most accurate model and among the lowest in energy drift on both
systems. On the chaotic free-swing task the GP gives by far the longest valid
prediction time ($>\!2.5\times$ the MLP) despite larger one-step error: smoothing
toward the GP prior damps the error growth that destabilises the MLP. The LGP

making it the best all-round model on clean dynamics; on the Pendubot it more than
doubles the LNN's valid time. The Polynomial baseline is weakest, with large drift
and $16$--$36\%$ divergence.

\subsection{The HNN failure is the assumption, not the formulation}
The naive HNN is the worst structured model: poor one-step accuracy, the fewest
valid steps, and $20$--$32\%$ divergence (its huge drift values are an artifact of
these divergences). Table~\ref{tab:hnn} isolates the cause. Replacing the
identity-mass assumption with a learned mass matrix---the Structured HNN, which by
Legendre duality has the same equations of motion as the LNN---removes the failure
entirely: divergence drops to $0\%$ and valid steps and drift match the LNN. The
naive HNN's failure is therefore due to the violated identity-mass assumption, not
to the Hamiltonian formulation. This is the practical lesson of the paper in
miniature: a structured prior helps only when its structural assumptions hold.

\subsection{Robustness to noise}
Table~\ref{tab:noise} reports the clean$\to$noisy change for both systems at
$n{=}4000$. All models degrade gracefully---one-step error roughly doubles---but
two effects stand out. The GP remains the most stable rollout model in absolute
terms. The LGP's GP residual, by contrast, \emph{overfits} the noise: its valid
prediction time collapses (Acrobot $52.9\!\to\!36.4$, Pendubot $58.6\!\to\!32.7$),
erasing the clean-condition advantage that made it the best all-rounder. Hard
structural priors (LNN) are comparatively noise-tolerant, even gaining valid steps
as the injected noise acts as mild regularization. The practical implication is
that a learned residual is a liability under observation noise, whereas a pure
structural prior degrades benignly.

\subsection{Downstream swing-up control}
\label{sec:control}
We drive an energy-shaping controller on the \emph{true} plant using a model's
energy estimate,
\begin{equation}
\label{eq:control}
u = k_e\,(E_{\mathrm{top}}-E_{\mathrm{model}}(x))\,\dot q_{\mathrm{act}}
   - k_p\,q_{\mathrm{act}} - k_d\,\dot q_{\mathrm{act}},
\end{equation}
clipped to a torque limit, where $E_{\mathrm{top}}$ is the upright energy under the
\emph{same} model (so any constant energy offset cancels) and $q_{\mathrm{act}}$ is
the actuated joint. The law pumps energy toward the upright level while damping the
actuated joint. Only models with an energy function (LNN, Structured HNN, Naive
HNN, LGP) can drive it; the MLP, Polynomial and GP cannot, which is itself a
result. We report the mean normalised tip height reached ($0$ down, $1$ upright)---
a continuous, robust measure of swing-up quality---and a success rate, with a
true-energy Oracle as the ceiling (Table~\ref{tab:control}, Fig.~\ref{fig:control}).

Models with an accurate energy (LNN, LGP, Structured HNN) swing the tip to
$\sim\!65\%$ of full height, well above the naive HNN, whose unreliable energy
yields the worst control; the black-box models cannot attempt the task at all. No
learned model matches the Oracle, and robustly \emph{balancing} the underactuated
systems at the top---which requires an LQR catch fed by a precise, low-velocity
swing-up entry---remains hard; we therefore treat the continuous tip-height as the
primary control metric and provide an optional LQR catch in the released code. The
ranking nonetheless tracks energy-model quality, directly connecting predictive
consistency to downstream usefulness.

\section{Discussion: A Map of When Physics Priors Help}
Across the four axes a consistent picture emerges.
\emph{First}, physics priors help most when their structure matches the system:
the LNN's learned positive-definite, configuration-dependent mass matrix is the
single most valuable ingredient, driving its wins on accuracy, energy and control,
and the Structured-HNN ablation shows the same structure rescues a failing model.
\emph{Second}, a mismatched prior is actively harmful---the naive HNN's
identity-mass assumption makes it worse than a black box and unstable.
\emph{Third}, the benefit of any prior is largest with scarce data and on
long-horizon, energy-sensitive tasks; at high data with only one-step accuracy in
view, the black-box MLP is competitive, at a fraction of the training cost.
\emph{Fourth}, uncertainty-aware models (GPs) buy rollout stability and data
efficiency complementary to hard priors, and combining the two (the LGP) is best on
clean dynamics---but its residual overfits noise, a robustness cost a practitioner
must weigh. No single model wins everything; the practical takeaway is to match the
prior to the data regime, horizon, noise level, and downstream task.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
Our study is in simulation on two planar systems with a fixed noise level. Absolute
swing-up success is modest because robustly balancing underactuated systems is a
hard control problem in its own right; a model-predictive controller would let
black-box models also be evaluated downstream and would better exploit accurate
one-step predictions. The exact GP is capped in training-set size and is
near-deterministic at the largest budget. Natural extensions include additional
systems (e.g.\ cart-pole), real-robot data, a sweep over noise levels, sparse or
deep-kernel GPs to scale the GP and LGP, and propagating GP/ensemble uncertainty
into the controller.

\section{Conclusion}
We presented a controlled, reproducible benchmark of seven dynamics models on two
underactuated systems across four axes, five seeds, three data budgets, and clean
and noisy conditions. Rather than crowning a single method, our results map out
\emph{when} physics priors help: most when the prior matches the system's
structure, when data are scarce, and on long-horizon, energy-sensitive tasks---
while a mismatched prior can be worse than none, and a learned residual can be a
liability under noise. We release the pipeline as a reusable testbed for
physics-informed dynamics learning.

A Controlled Benchmark of Physics-Informed Dynamics Models\\

\begin{abstract}
Physics-informed machine learning promises data-efficient, physically
consistent dynamics models, but it is often unclear \emph{when} a physics prior
pays off relative to a black-box baseline---or whether a mismatched prior can
hurt. We present a controlled, reproducible benchmark of seven learned dynamics
models---a multilayer perceptron (MLP), a polynomial system-identification
baseline, a Gaussian process (GP), a Lagrangian Neural Network (LNN), a naive
Hamiltonian Neural Network (HNN), a structured HNN with a learned mass matrix,
and a Lagrangian-informed GP (LGP)---on two underactuated systems, the Acrobot
and the Pendubot. We evaluate four axes: one-step accuracy, long-horizon rollout
stability (valid prediction time), energy drift, and downstream energy-based
swing-up control, across five seeds, four data budgets, and both clean and
sensor/process-noise conditions. We find a nuanced map rather than a single
winner. The LNN, whose learned positive-definite mass matrix matches these
systems, is the most accurate and energy-consistent and never diverges. The GP
is the most data-efficient and yields the most stable rollouts despite modest
one-step accuracy. The LGP blends the two, achieving LNN-level accuracy with
GP-level rollout stability and the lowest energy drift. The naive HNN, whose
identity-mass assumption is violated by configuration-dependent inertia, is
worse than the black-box MLP and diverges in $20$--$32\%$ of rollouts; a
controlled ablation shows that giving the Hamiltonian model a learned mass
matrix---which by Legendre duality recovers the LNN---fully removes this failure,
isolating the assumption rather than the formulation as the cause. Under noise
all models degrade gracefully, but the GP residual in the LGP overfits noise and
loses rollout stability. Only models with an energy function can drive
energy-based control. The result is a concrete map of when physics priors help:
scarce data, long horizons and energy-sensitive tasks, and a prior whose
structure matches the system.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
Learning the dynamics of a mechanical system from data underpins model-based
control and reinforcement learning. Plain neural networks fit one-step
transitions accurately, yet rolled out over long horizons they drift and violate
invariants such as energy conservation. Physics-informed machine learning (PiML)
bakes structure---Lagrangian or Hamiltonian mechanics, positive-definite
inertia, energy conservation---into the model \cite{karniadakis2021,raissi2019},
promising better long-horizon behaviour and sample efficiency.

In practice it is rarely obvious \emph{when} a prior is worth its complexity, or
whether a prior whose assumptions are violated by the real system can hurt. Most
papers introduce one physics-informed model and show it beats a baseline on a
favourable task; what is missing is a controlled, like-for-like comparison across
priors, evaluation criteria, data budgets, and noise conditions.

We provide such a comparison and summarise it as a map of when physics priors
help, with four findings:
\begin{enumerate}
\item \textbf{Matching structure matters most.} The LNN, which learns a
positive-definite, configuration-dependent mass matrix, is the most accurate and
energy-consistent model and never diverges.
\item \textbf{A mismatched prior is worse than none.} The naive HNN, assuming an
identity mass matrix, is worse than the black-box MLP and diverges in
$20$--$32\%$ of rollouts. A controlled ablation pinpoints the assumption: giving
the Hamiltonian model a learned mass matrix removes the failure.
\item \textbf{Uncertainty buys stability and data efficiency.} The GP is the most
data-efficient model and yields the most stable rollouts; combining it with
Lagrangian structure (the LGP) gives the best overall blend.
\item \textbf{Robustness has costs.} Under noise the GP is the most robust, while
the LGP's GP residual overfits noise and loses rollout stability.
\end{enumerate}
All experiments are reproducible from a released, configuration-driven pipeline.

\section{Related Work}
\textbf{Physics-informed neural dynamics.} Deep Lagrangian Networks (DeLaN)
\cite{lutter2019} learn the mass matrix and potential of a Lagrangian and recover
the equations of motion by automatic differentiation; Lagrangian Neural Networks
\cite{cranmer2020} generalise the idea. Hamiltonian Neural Networks
\cite{greydanus2019} learn a Hamiltonian and integrate the symplectic flow,
conserving energy but requiring canonical coordinates; symplectic and
variational-integrator variants extend them to control and rigid bodies
\cite{zhong2020,saemundsson2020}. Physics-informed neural networks more broadly
embed governing equations into the loss \cite{raissi2019,karniadakis2021}.

\textbf{Gaussian-process and classical dynamics.} GPs provide calibrated
uncertainty and data efficiency for model learning \cite{rasmussen2006,nguyen2011}
and underpin sample-efficient control such as PILCO \cite{deisenroth2011}. Sparse
regression on a feature library (SINDy-style) is a classical system-ID baseline
\cite{brunton2016}. Hybrid schemes learn a data-driven residual on top of a
parametric or structured model.

\textbf{Underactuated swing-up.} The Acrobot and Pendubot are standard
benchmarks; energy-shaping controllers swing them toward upright by regulating
total energy, typically with an LQR catch for final balancing
\cite{spong1995,tedrake}. We use swing-up as a downstream task that stresses the
\emph{usefulness} of a learned model, not only its predictive error.

We hold data, integrator, training objective, and evaluation fixed across all
models, enabling a fair comparison rather than a single favourable demonstration.

\section{Systems and Problem Setup}
We study two planar double pendulums with a single actuator, sharing the
manipulator equation
\begin{equation}
M(q)\,\ddot q + C(q,\dot q)\,\dot q + g(q) = B\,u,
\end{equation}
with joint angles $q=(q_1,q_2)$, mass matrix $M(q)\succ 0$, Coriolis term $C$,
gravity $g(q)=\partial V/\partial q$, and actuation matrix $B$. The
\textbf{Acrobot} is actuated only at the elbow ($B=[0,1]^\top$) and the
\textbf{Pendubot} only at the shoulder ($B=[1,0]^\top$); the other joint is
passive. The state is $x=(q_1,q_2,\dot q_1,\dot q_2)$ and $u$ is a scalar torque;
we integrate with RK4 at $\Delta t=10^{-2}\,$s. Given transitions
$(x_t,u_t,x_{t+1})$ under random torques, the task is to learn a model that
predicts accelerations, hence the next state via the same integrator. A single
integrator and one-step objective are used for every model, so behavioural
differences are attributable to the prior.

\section{The Model Zoo}
All models map $(x,u)$ to a state derivative and are rolled out with RK4.

\textbf{Vanilla MLP (black box).} Regresses joint accelerations directly; no
physical structure.

\textbf{Polynomial (system ID).} Closed-form ridge regression of accelerations on
a degree-2 polynomial expansion of $[\sin q_1,\cos q_1,\sin q_2,\cos q_2,\dot
q_1,\dot q_2,u]$; a cheap classical baseline.

\textbf{Gaussian Process (GP).} An exact GP with an ARD-RBF kernel regresses each
acceleration component from the same features, fit by marginal likelihood; the
$(\sin,\cos)$ encoding respects periodicity. We cap the training set at $1000$
points for tractability.

\textbf{Lagrangian Neural Network (LNN).} Two networks output a Cholesky factor of
$M(q)=L(q)L(q)^\top+\epsilon I\succ 0$ and a potential $V(q)$; the Coriolis and
gravity terms follow by automatic differentiation and the acceleration from
$M\ddot q = Bu - C\dot q - g$. The known $B$ is supplied as a prior.

\textbf{Naive HNN.} A scalar Hamiltonian $H(q,p)$ with the identity-mass
convention $p:=\dot q$, integrated along the symplectic flow $\dot q=\partial
H/\partial p,\ \dot p=-\partial H/\partial q + Bu$. It does \emph{not} learn a
configuration-dependent mass matrix.

\textbf{Structured HNN.} A Hamiltonian model that \emph{learns} $M(q)$ and $V(q)$,
with canonical momentum $p=M(q)\dot q$. By the Legendre transform its equations of
motion coincide with the LNN's; we include it as a controlled ablation of the
naive HNN's identity-mass assumption.

\textbf{Lagrangian-informed GP (LGP).} A hybrid: an LNN supplies a physics mean
and a GP models the residual acceleration, $\ddot q = \ddot q_{\mathrm{LNN}} +
\mathrm{GP}_{\mathrm{res}}$, keeping structure while letting the GP correct what
the structural model misses.

The structured networks conserve their own learned energy to $\sim\!10^{-7}$ over
$200$ steps before training, confirming the autograd equations of motion.

\section{Experimental Setup}
\textbf{Data.} Per system we generate $8000$ transitions from random rollouts; a
$20\%$ tail is held out for one-step evaluation and the pool is subsampled to
budgets of $250,1000,4000$ to probe data efficiency. The noisy condition adds
process noise (a random state kick each step) and sensor noise (added to recorded
states), both with std $0.02$.

\textbf{Training.} Each model minimises a one-step angle-aware MSE between the
RK4-integrated prediction and the recorded next state (GP-style models are fit by
marginal likelihood). We use $100$ epochs, Adam, gradient clipping, and
\emph{five} seeds per configuration.

\textbf{Axes.} (1)~one-step MSE; (2)~\emph{valid prediction time}, the steps a
free-swing rollout tracks the truth before the per-step error exceeds a threshold
(robust on chaotic systems, where raw long-horizon MSE saturates); (3)~energy
drift over the rollout, plus the fraction of rollouts that diverge;
(4)~swing-up control (Section~\ref{sec:control}). Rollouts use identical fixed
initial conditions across models.

\textbf{Accuracy and energy.} The LNN is the most accurate model on both systems
and among the lowest in energy drift. The Polynomial baseline is weakest, with
large drift and $16$--$36\%$ divergence. The MLP is a competent middle performer.

\textbf{Stability.} On the chaotic free-swing task the GP gives by far the
longest valid prediction time ($>\!2.5\times$ the MLP) despite larger one-step
error: GP smoothing damps the error growth that destabilises the MLP. The LGP
combines LNN-level accuracy with high valid time and the lowest energy drift,
making it the best all-round model on the dynamics axes.

\textbf{Data efficiency} (Fig.~\ref{fig:sweep}). At the smallest budget the GP and
Lagrangian models are already far more accurate than the MLP, whose advantage
only appears with more data; physics priors and GP smoothing help most when data
are scarce.

\subsection{The HNN Failure Is the Assumption, Not the Formulation}
The naive HNN is the worst structured model: poor one-step accuracy, the fewest
valid steps, and $20$--$32\%$ divergence (its huge drift values are an artifact
of these divergences). Table~\ref{tab:hnn} isolates the cause. Replacing the
identity-mass assumption with a learned mass matrix---the Structured HNN, which by
Legendre duality has the same equations of motion as the LNN---removes the failure
entirely: divergence drops to $0\%$ and valid steps and drift match the LNN. The
naive HNN's failure is therefore due to the violated identity-mass assumption,
not to the Hamiltonian formulation.

\subsection{Robustness to Noise}
Table~\ref{tab:noise} reports the clean$\to$noisy change at $n{=}4000$. All
models degrade gracefully (one-step error roughly doubles). Two effects stand
out: the GP remains the most stable in absolute terms, while the LGP's GP
residual \emph{overfits} the noise and loses rollout stability (valid steps fall
from $52.9$ to $36.4$ on the Acrobot), eroding its clean-condition advantage.
Hard structural priors (LNN) are comparatively noise-tolerant.

\subsection{Downstream Swing-Up Control (Axis 4)}
\label{sec:control}
We drive an energy-shaping controller on the \emph{true} plant using a model's
energy estimate,
\begin{equation}
u = k_e\,(E_{\mathrm{top}}-E_{\mathrm{model}}(x))\,\dot q_{\mathrm{act}}
   - k_p\,q_{\mathrm{act}} - k_d\,\dot q_{\mathrm{act}},
\end{equation}
clipped to a torque limit, with $E_{\mathrm{top}}$ the upright energy under the
same model. Only models with an energy function (LNN, Structured HNN, Naive HNN,
LGP) can drive it; the MLP, Polynomial and GP cannot, which is itself a result.
We report the mean normalised tip height reached ($0$ down, $1$ upright)---a
continuous, robust measure of swing-up quality---and a success rate, with a
true-energy Oracle as the ceiling (Table~\ref{tab:control}, Fig.~\ref{fig:control}).

Models with an accurate energy (LNN, LGP, Structured HNN) swing the tip to
$\sim\!65\%$ of full height, well above the naive HNN; black-box models cannot
attempt the task. No learned model matches the Oracle, and robustly
\emph{balancing} the underactuated systems at the top---which requires an LQR
catch fed by precise swing-up entry---remains hard; we therefore treat the
continuous tip-height as the primary control metric and provide an optional LQR
catch in the released code. The ranking nonetheless tracks energy-model quality,
connecting predictive accuracy to downstream usefulness.

\section{Discussion: A Map of When Physics Priors Help}
Across the four axes a consistent picture emerges. \emph{First}, physics priors
help most when their structure matches the system: the LNN's learned
positive-definite, configuration-dependent mass matrix is the single most valuable
ingredient, and the Structured-HNN ablation shows the same structure recovers a
failing model. \emph{Second}, a mismatched prior is actively harmful---the naive
HNN's identity-mass assumption makes it worse than a black box and unstable.
\emph{Third}, the benefit of any prior is largest with scarce data and on
long-horizon, energy-sensitive tasks; at high data with only one-step accuracy in
view, the MLP is competitive. \emph{Fourth}, uncertainty-aware models (GPs) buy
rollout stability and data efficiency complementary to hard priors, and combining
them (the LGP) is best on clean dynamics---but its residual overfits noise, a
robustness cost. No single model wins everything, which is the practical guidance
a practitioner needs.

\section{Limitations and Future Work}
Our study is in simulation on two planar systems. Absolute swing-up success is
modest because robust balancing of underactuated systems is a hard control
problem in its own right; per-model model-predictive control would also let
black-box models be evaluated downstream. The exact GP is capped in training-set
size, and is near-deterministic at the largest budget. Extensions include
additional systems (e.g.\ cart-pole), real-robot data, larger noise sweeps, and
sparse or deep-kernel GPs to scale the GP and LGP.

\section{Conclusion}
We presented a controlled, reproducible benchmark of seven dynamics models on two
underactuated systems across four axes, five seeds, and clean and noisy
conditions. Rather than crowning a method, our results map out \emph{when} physics
priors help: most when the prior matches the system's structure, when data are
scarce, and on long-horizon, energy-sensitive tasks---while a mismatched prior can
be worse than none. We release the pipeline as a reusable testbed.
